Vimy Ridge

Overview

Looking back...

In the past, France has already tried taking Vimy Ridge. It was a high priority target yet, despite this, all previous attempts were unsuccessful. Over 100,000 French troops attempted to take the ridge and lost to the Germans only numbering 15,000.

3 years after the beginning of WW1...

The Canadian Corps would fight against the German forces. At this point, soldiers were running out of resources and the war was needing to end. For Canadians, the goal was to take Vimy Ridge away from the Germans. Unlike previous battles, there was a lot of preparation involved. Specific roles for the battle were assigned and were trained specifically for the terrain.

The battle begins on April 9, 1917 at 5:30 AM...

All four of the Canadian divisions attacked the German held ridge. Led by General Arthur Currie, tunnellers, engineers, and railway troops all worked together. Canadian troops would successfully storm the ridge despite facing significant opposition and heavily fortified German positions. Creeping barrage was used and helped Canada tremendously. The battle lasted for a total of 3 days and the Canadians achieved victory on April 12.

Data: 15,000 casualties for a territory that was 9 km long and 8 km wide.

How was it defining?

Many historians give credit to the Battle of Vimy Ridge to be the true “Birth of Canada”. It showed other nations what Canadians were capable of. This showed national identity and unity. The Battle of Vimy Ridge marked the first time that all four divisions of the Canadian Corps fought together as a unified force. The success of capturing the ridge instilled a sense of national pride and identity, helping to solidify Canada’s eruption as an independent nation.

Relevance

The Battle of Vimy Ridge introduced new technological and tactical innovation as it witnessed the application of new strategies including the creeping barrage and the use of underground tunnels. The Canadian success at Vimy Ridge showed their ability to adapt and innovate on the battlefield.

Vimy Ridge is still relevant today as it continues to serve as a site of remembrance and commemoration, honouring the sacrifices made by Canadian soldiers all throughout World War I. It reminds future generations of the cost of war. Also, the victory at Vimy Ridge served to influence and impact Canadian military culture, emphasising qualities including bravery, teamwork, and innovation.
