The Person's Case

Overview

Also known as the Valiant Five and the Alberta Five, the Famous Five established the right for women to vote and serve as elected officials in the 1920s. Together they have set and advocated towards changing life for both women and children.

The group consisted of Emily Murphy, Henrietta Muir Edwards, Nellie McClug, Lousie Crummy McKinney, and Irene Parlby.

1867

The British North American Act is signed. Women are not ‘persons’ based on terminology.

1916

Emily Murphy becomes the first female judge.

1917

Both men and women are classified as persons in Alberta.

1917-1927

Emily Murphy decides she wants to become the first female Senator and battles for the position. She was frequently defeated by the Act from 1867.

1927

Emily Murphy’s brother, who was a lawyer, suggested appealing the Act through Section 60 which stated that to gain clarification on a constitutional point through the Federal Cabinet, there needed to be five citizens acting as a unit to appeal.

1928

As a result of their actions, the Supreme Court considers the question of whether ‘persons’ included females, which they ended up saying no to.

1929

The Famous 5 bring in Prime Minister Mackenzie King for moral and financial support and bring their case to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council of England (the highest court of appeal).

October 18, 1929

Lord Sankey, Lord Chancellor of the all male Privy Council announces that “Yes, women are persons…and are eligible to be summoned”.

How was it defining?

The Person’s Case was a defining moment for Canada as it was a direct advancement of women’s rights. The Person’s Case challenged the idea that women were not considered as “persons” in a legal sense. The decision of the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council recognized women as persons under the law, marking a significant step forward for women’s rights. It broadened the understanding of legal terms and highlighted the need for equality in the country’s constitutional framework.

Relevance

The Person’s Case is still relevant today as public perception was forever changed along with a greater equality achieved. The appearance and actions of the Famous Five motivate participation of women in public and political life to increase. The activism of the Famous Five serves as a milestone in Canada’s progress towards equal rights and opportunities for women. It provides a foundation for arguments in favour of gender equality and helps guide future legal challenges related to women’s rights.
